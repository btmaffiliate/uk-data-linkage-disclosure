# Point 1 — The Threshold-Splitting Question
## How a £500,000 Palantir programme was run as two sub-threshold contracts — and escaped MOPAC oversight

**Prepared for:** the Office of Iqbal Mohamed MP (Dewsbury and Batley)
**By:** Brandon Myers, independent public-interest researcher — brandonmyers.net
**Date:** June 2026
**Status:** Built from public sources. Confirmed facts, the questions they raise, and what still needs confirming are kept separate throughout. This is analysis for parliamentary scrutiny, not a legal opinion or an allegation of fact against any named person.

---

## 1. The point, in one paragraph

The Metropolitan Police ran its Palantir "**Unified Data Platform (UDP)**" programme **not as one contract but as two** — an **initial pilot of approximately £10,001** and a further contract of **£489,999** — which **together total £500,000: the exact level at which, under the MOPAC Scheme of Delegation and Consent, the Deputy Mayor for Policing and Crime must approve the contract and publish a decision.** Neither contract, taken alone, crossed the line; **aggregated, they sit precisely on it.** The effect — whatever the intent — is that a live, controversial Palantir deployment that profiles the force's own officers proceeded **without the Deputy Mayor's approval, without a published Policing and Crime Decision, and without the public and London Assembly scrutiny that a £500,000 contract attracts.** This is not a contract that came in a pound under a line; it is **a single programme divided into two sub-threshold contracts** — and dividing a procurement to keep it below an oversight threshold is the precise conduct that the Procurement Act 2023 prohibits. The narrow, answerable question for Parliament and City Hall: **why was one programme split into two contracts that individually escaped MOPAC approval but together reach exactly £500,000 — and was that subdivision deliberate?**

## 2. The confirmed fact pattern

**[confirmed — figures to confirm against the primary notices] The programme was split into two sub-threshold contracts.** The UDP appears on Contracts Finder under "Unified Data Platform Phase 1." Rather than a single contract, the work was run as **two contracts — an initial pilot of approximately £10,001 and a further contract of £489,999 — which together total £500,000**, the exact level at which MOPAC approval is mandatory. Each contract individually sits below the £500,000 line; only their **aggregate** reaches it. The reporting characterises the £489,999 element as having *"avoided hitting the £500,000 threshold for scrutiny from the Mayor's Office for Policing and Crime."* The pilot originally ran **1 February – 30 April 2026** and was **extended to 15 May 2026**; the Met declined to disclose the extension's cost, calling it *"commercially sensitive"* — so the *true* aggregate may be higher still.
*Source: The Nerve, "New fears over spread of Palantir's influence…", May 2026 — https://www.thenerve.news/p/met-police-palantir-officer-ai-surveillance-misconduct-extension-contract-federation (attributes the figures to the Met's own Contracts Finder notices). **The exact value of each of the two contracts should be confirmed against the primary Contracts Finder notices before being stated as unqualified fact — see §7.**)*

**[confirmed] What the pilot does.** The UDP is **not** a crime database. It is a professional-standards / "continuous integrity screening" pilot that brings the Met's **own internally-held employee data** into Palantir Foundry to flag potential misconduct — analysing officers' **sickness and absence, overtime patterns, declared secondary employment, and data from work mobile phones and laptops including device geolocation.** The Met frames it as data it "already lawfully holds"; the Metropolitan Police Federation called it *"outrageous,"* citing "continuous 24/7 geolocation tracking."
*Sources: Met Police news (Apr 2026) — https://news.met.police.uk/news/metropolitan-police-strengthens-standards-using-technology-and-new-vetting-powers-508631 ; Silicon UK — https://www.silicon.co.uk/e-regulation/surveillance/met-police-palantir-629642 ; The Nerve (as above).*

That contract **was** put to MOPAC and was **refused by the Deputy Mayor for Policing and Crime (Kaya Comer-Schwartz) on/around 20–21 May 2026**, citing procurement-process failings (the Met having "only engaged with one potential supplier"). Palantir is reported to be moving to sue.

**The contrast is the story:** the **£50m** deal that went through the front door was **stopped** for engaging only one supplier; the **£489,999** pilot — same supplier, same family of technology — went ahead **below the threshold that would have triggered the same scrutiny.**

## 3. Why £489,999 is the number that matters: the MOPAC oversight threshold

**[confirmed] £500,000 is a hard governance line.** Under the **MOPAC Scheme of Delegation and Consent (Paragraph 4.8)**, the **Deputy Mayor for Policing and Crime** holds delegated authority to approve, among other things, **the award of contracts with a total value of £500,000 or above**, requests to **go out to tender** at £500,000 or above, and **business cases** for expenditure at that level. Decisions at or above the threshold are taken as **published Policing and Crime Decisions (PCDs)** on the London City Hall website — the mechanism by which the public, the press and the **London Assembly Police and Crime Committee** see and can question MPS contracts.
*Source: MOPAC Scheme of Delegation and Consent; MOPAC published decisions (PCDs) — https://www.london.gov.uk/programmes-strategies/mayors-office-policing-and-crime-mopac/mopac-governance-and-decision-making/mopac-decisions*

**The consequence of splitting the programme.** Run as two contracts that individually fall below £500,000 — but together reach it — the programme:
- requires **no Deputy Mayor approval** (each contract is treated as sub-threshold);
- generates **no published PCD** — so there is **no public decision document**, no stated rationale, no recorded risk assessment in the public domain;
- escapes the routine **London Assembly** visibility that PCDs provide;
- and, because the *original* value was below £500,000, even the scheme's rule that **extensions** to £500,000-plus contracts need approval (where the extension exceeds 10% or 12 months) **does not bite** — which is why the undisclosed extension could be added without triggering scrutiny either.

In other words, splitting one programme into a ~£10,001 contract and a £489,999 contract does not merely shave a pound off a total. It **switches off the entire public-accountability apparatus** that a single £500,000 contract would have engaged — which is exactly why the law treats *aggregate* value, not the value of each slice, as what counts.

## 4. The procurement-law dimension: structuring to stay under a threshold is prohibited

**[confirmed — legal framework] Authorities must not manipulate or subdivide value to dodge thresholds.** The pilot ran in 2026, under the **Procurement Act 2023** (in force from 24 February 2025). **Section 4** requires a contracting authority to estimate a contract's value using the Schedule 3 methodology and **restricts manipulation of the estimated value to avoid the Act's requirements**. **Schedule 3, paragraph 4** is an express **anti-avoidance** rule: authorities **must not artificially subdivide a procurement** to evade the rules, and must estimate the **maximum** value payable **including options to extend or renew**. Government guidance is explicit that *"rule-avoidance would never be a good reason"* to depart from aggregation.
*Sources: GOV.UK, Procurement Act 2023 guidance — "Valuation of Contracts" (https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/procurement-act-2023-guidance-documents-define-phase/guidance-valuation-of-contracts-html) and "Thresholds" (https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/procurement-act-2023-guidance-documents-define-phase/guidance-thresholds-html). The predecessor rule was PCR 2015 reg. 6(3).*

**Two points follow, and they are questions, not conclusions:**
1. The anti-avoidance principle in national procurement law and the MOPAC £500,000 governance line are **distinct** (different figures, different purposes), but they share one logic: **you may not set or split a contract's value to escape the controls that would otherwise apply.** A flagship AI pilot landing at exactly **£1** under the local oversight threshold is precisely the fact-pattern those rules exist to catch.
2. **This is not a borderline case — it is two contracts for one programme.** A ~£10,001 pilot and a £489,999 contract for the same "Unified Data Platform," summing to £500,000, is on its face an **artificial subdivision**: the law required the authority to estimate the **aggregate maximum value** of the programme (including the undisclosed extension), which reaches — and with the extension likely exceeds — £500,000 and engages MOPAC approval. The Met and MOPAC can answer from their own records whether these were properly treated as one procurement; if they were one programme, splitting them is the conduct Schedule 3 paragraph 4 exists to prevent.

## 5. Why it warrants "going to town" — the propriety questions

- **Oversight was bypassed on the most sensitive possible subject.** This is not stationery. It is AI surveillance of police officers' health, movements and devices, on a US national-security contractor's platform — exactly the kind of high-risk processing for which public scrutiny exists — and it proceeded with none.
- **The pattern looks like a foot in the door.** A sub-threshold "Phase 1" pilot, then a ~£50m "Unified Operational Analytics" deal. The small one slipped through below the line; the big one was caught and blocked for single-supplier procurement. That sequencing is itself a legitimate scrutiny target.
- **Commercial-sensitivity was used to withhold the extension cost** — the very figure that would show whether the *total* crossed £500,000.
- **No published DPIA, ROPA or ethical review** exists for the pilot (covered in the main pack), so the threshold avoidance also removed the document trail that a PCD would normally have surfaced.
- **The Nolan principles** (openness, accountability) and the public-sector duty of transparency are engaged whether or not any rule was formally broken.

## 6. The questions to put, and where

**To the Mayor / MOPAC (Mayor's Question Time; London Assembly Police & Crime Committee):**
1. **How many separate contracts** were entered into with Palantir for the "Unified Data Platform," what was the **value of each**, and what was the **combined total including any extension** — and why was the programme not let as a single contract?
2. Given that the contracts together total (at least) £500,000, was the value **estimated in accordance with Schedule 3 of the Procurement Act 2023** (which requires aggregation and prohibits artificial subdivision), and on what basis was each contract treated as a separate, below-threshold procurement?
3. Why was a Palantir surveillance pilot of this sensitivity **not** brought to the Deputy Mayor for approval, and **no Policing and Crime Decision published**?
4. Were the UDP pilot and the (blocked) £50m **Unified Operational Analytics** proposal treated as **separate** procurements, and if so, why was aggregation not required?

**Written Parliamentary Questions (Home Office / Cabinet Office):**
5. What guidance exists to prevent contracting authorities **structuring AI/data-platform contracts to fall below local oversight thresholds**, and how is compliance with the Procurement Act 2023 anti-avoidance rule monitored for police technology?

**FOI (to the Met and to MOPAC):**
6. The Contracts Finder notice and contract for "Unified Data Platform Phase 1"; the value of the extension; any internal value-estimation/threshold assessment; and any legal/commercial advice on whether MOPAC approval was required.

**ICO:** whether it has been notified of, or has reviewed, the processing of officers' special-category and location data under the pilot.
