Your health record, your tax record, your benefits record, your justice record — held by different departments, with no shared ID between them. You probably assume they stay separate.

Increasingly, they don't.

I spent the last few days documenting how several governments now use **Splink** — an open-source probabilistic record-linkage tool, originally built by the UK Ministry of Justice — to join previously-siloed citizen datasets into single, person-level linked records.

What I found, built entirely from the governments' own published documents:

• **UK Ministry of Justice** links people across courts, prisons and probation into one cross-justice identity — and is piloting real-time identification of individuals as they appear in court.
• **UK ONS** linked the 2021 Census to DWP and HMRC records (96.7% matched).
• **NHS England** is implementing Splink as the engine for a new patient-data linkage service.
• **Australia's ABS** built its 2025 Person Linkage Spine on Splink — joining Medicare, Centrelink and tax records across three federal agencies.

To be fair about it: this is lawful. The statistical method is sound and accurate. Real safeguards exist (Five Safes, accredited-researcher schemes, de-identification). And there are genuine public benefits — census accuracy, health research, fewer duplicate records.

But across every deployment I reviewed, the public documents cite a legal basis almost no citizen has heard of — and **not one mentions individual consent, notification, or any way to opt out.** The linkage is invisible to the person it's about. And it's quietly drifting from *statistics* toward *operational identification of named individuals.*

I think that deserves daylight — and a conversation that citizens are actually part of.
